Luck and Pluck

Talent vs. luck

An interactive experience on the shape and reasons of social inequality

Social inequality is a source of a great deal of political arguments, and has been for quite a while. For some, the very existence of differences in lifestyle as big as the ones between a minimum wage worker in a restaurant and the CEO of a big corporation is a fundamental injustice. Other argue however that this difference only reflects a much more natural one: a difference in talent, or hard work, between the two.

In February 2018, a paper was published studying this very problem with a simple mathematical model. Specifically, the paper focuses on the source of success, and whether it is true that the best get farther, or if it's not rather just a matter of chance. The paper is title "Talent vs. Luck: the role of randomness in success and failure", by A. Pluchino, A.E. Biondo, and A. Rapisarda, and can be downloaded here. If you know a bit of mathematics, it's a pretty easy read.

Ok, but... what does it say?

It says that, if we make some reasonable assumptions, the amount of individual talent and hard work (our so-called pluck - yes, it's a Jojo reference) is not enough to explain the shape of the distribution of wealth in our world. Rather, the distribution that we observe can only be justified by a fair amount of luck. In other words, rich people aren't most likely all rich because they're that good at whatever it is that they do. A lot of them are probably pretty average; they ended up being rich because good things just happened to them by chance.

Wait, shape of the distribution? What is that?

It's the mathematical way that we use to describe how likely it is to find a member of a certain group having a certain quality. A curve that connects the value of that quality (for example, a certain personal worth) to the likelihood of finding someone who has that value (for example, the likelihood of picking a random person and finding them having a personal worth higher than $1M is obviously tiny).

For example, this is called a normal distribution. It represents things where a lot of people have so-and-so values (the peak in the middle), but very few people have either very low or very high ones (the tails at the left and right extremes). This is a typical distribution for IQ: a lot of normal people, a few geniuses and a few complete idiots.

This, instead, is called a Pareto distribution.
